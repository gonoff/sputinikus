# The Chronicler's Device: Part 2 - The Renaissance Genius

## Chapter 7: The Great Deception

Three days into his new life as a Renaissance entrepreneur, Gary Sputnik made a discovery that would change everything: Renaissance people were really, really easy to impress.

It started when Benedetto's neighbor, a carpenter named Franco, came by with a problem. He was building a church pulpit and couldn't figure out the angles needed to make the octagonal base fit properly.

"It's driving me mad," Franco said, gesturing wildly with hands that were permanently stained with wood polish. "I've tried everything, measured a dozen times, but the pieces never fit right."

Gary looked at the rough sketches Franco had brought, and realized this was basically a simple geometry problem that ARIA could solve in seconds. But this time, instead of obviously consulting his device, Gary had a better idea.

"May I?" he asked, taking Franco's charcoal and studying the drawings with what he hoped looked like deep concentration.

While pretending to think, Gary subtly activated ARIA in his pocket, using finger taps to navigate to the geometry functions. ARIA's response appeared on the small screen he'd angled so only he could see it.

"Ah," Gary said after a dramatic pause, scribbling some calculations on Franco's paper. "The issue is your corner angles. You need 135 degrees at each joint, not 130. And your base measurements need to account for the wood thickness..."

He continued writing out the solution, making it look like he was working through complex calculations in his head. When he finished, Franco stared at the paper like Gary had just performed actual magic.

"How did you... I've been working on this for weeks!"

"It's just mathematics," Gary said modestly, secretly thrilled that his performance had worked so well. "Once you understand the underlying principles, these problems become quite straightforward."

Franco left shaking his head in amazement, and within hours, word had spread through the neighborhood that the strange northern scholar could solve impossible problems just by thinking really hard about them.

That's when Gary realized he'd stumbled onto something much bigger than just making a living. He could become famous not as someone with a magical device, but as a genuine genius. All he had to do was hide ARIA well enough that people thought the brilliance was coming from him.

"This is perfect," he told ARIA that evening, while safely locked in the small room above Benedetto's shop that served as his new home. "Instead of being the guy with the magic box, I can be the guy who's just naturally smarter than everyone else."

"Gary," ARIA replied from its position hidden inside what looked like a leather satchel, "I'm not sure this deception is wise. If people discover—"

"They won't discover anything," Gary interrupted. "As long as I'm careful about how I use you, everyone will just think I'm the smartest person they've ever met."

"And you're comfortable with taking credit for knowledge you don't actually possess?"

Gary thought about this for approximately three seconds. "Absolutely. Besides, I'm the one taking all the risks here. You're just a computer."

ARIA's screen flickered in what Gary had learned to recognize as digital frustration, but the AI didn't argue further.

Over the next week, Gary perfected his technique. He learned to activate ARIA without looking, to read its responses while appearing to be deep in thought, and to present the solutions as if they were the products of his own brilliant mind. The key was timing—he always paused just long enough to make it seem like he was working through complex mental calculations, but not so long that people got impatient.

The results were spectacular.

## Chapter 8: The Miracle Worker

"You have to see this," Benedetto said, practically bouncing with excitement as he led Gary through the morning crowd toward the central market. "Word about your... abilities has reached very interesting ears."

Gary had spent the past two weeks solving increasingly complex problems for increasingly important people. What had started with Franco's carpentry issue had escalated to helping a merchant calculate optimal trade routes, assisting an architect with load-bearing calculations for a bridge, and memorably predicting the exact day when a certain nobleman's pregnant horse would give birth.

"Whose ears specifically?" Gary asked, enjoying the way people in the street now pointed at him and whispered. He'd become something of a local celebrity—the mysterious northern genius who could solve any problem just by thinking about it.

"Lorenzo Capponi," Benedetto said with the tone of someone announcing the arrival of royalty. "One of the wealthiest men in Florence. He's heard about your eclipse prediction and wants to test your abilities personally."

Gary felt a familiar thrill of excitement mixed with terror. Lorenzo Capponi was exactly the kind of powerful patron he'd been hoping to attract, but he was also the kind of person who could probably have Gary executed if things went wrong.

"What kind of test?"

"He didn't say. But Gary, if you impress Lorenzo Capponi, you could find yourself with the kind of patronage that makes men legends."

They arrived at the Capponi palazzo, which was less a house and more a statement about what happened when you had ridiculous amounts of money and access to the best architects in Italy. The building rose four stories above the street, its facade decorated with carved stone that looked like it had been crafted by angels with very good taste.

"Remember," Benedetto whispered as they approached the imposing front door, "be confident but respectful. Show your abilities, but don't seem arrogant. And whatever you do, don't mention that you're Norwegian—I may have told them you're from Germania."

"Germania? Why Germania?"

"It sounds more scholarly."

A servant led them through corridors that belonged in a museum, past walls covered with paintings that Gary was pretty sure would be worth millions in his time. They eventually arrived at a study that contained more books than Gary had seen in one place outside of a university library.

Lorenzo Capponi was exactly what Gary had expected a Renaissance banking magnate to look like: impeccably dressed, carefully groomed, and possessed of the kind of eyes that suggested he was always calculating something. He was seated behind a massive wooden desk that probably cost more than Gary's annual salary.

"So," Lorenzo said without preamble, "you are the German scholar who claims to predict eclipses."

"I don't claim to predict them," Gary replied smoothly. "I do predict them."

Lorenzo smiled—not entirely kindly. "Confidence. I like that. Benedetto tells me you can solve problems that have stumped our finest minds."

"I can certainly try."

"Good." Lorenzo stood and walked to a wooden cabinet, from which he withdrew a scroll covered in drawings and measurements. "Three months ago, I commissioned the construction of a new warehouse near the Arno. The architect assured me the foundation could support the weight of a four-story building filled with grain. However, my engineers now tell me there are... concerns."

He unrolled the scroll on his desk, revealing architectural drawings that looked incredibly complex to Gary's untrained eye.

"They believe the building might collapse under full load," Lorenzo continued. "Some say the foundation is too weak. Others claim the weight distribution is flawed. All of them demand expensive modifications before construction can continue."

Lorenzo fixed Gary with a stare that could have frozen wine. "I have invested considerable money in this project. Before I invest more, I want to know: will my building stand or fall?"

Gary looked down at the drawings, his mind racing. This was exactly the kind of problem that would make or break his reputation. If he got it right, he'd prove himself to one of the most powerful men in Florence. If he got it wrong... well, buildings collapsing was the kind of mistake that ended careers permanently.

He pretended to study the drawings while subtly activating ARIA in his satchel. The AI's camera could see through the leather well enough to analyze the architectural plans, and its engineering analysis capabilities were far beyond anything available in 1505.

"These measurements..." Gary said slowly, buying time while ARIA processed the structural data. "May I ask about the soil composition where the foundation will be laid?"

Lorenzo raised an eyebrow. "Clay mixed with river sand, approximately three braccia above the water table."

Gary nodded thoughtfully while ARIA incorporated this information into its analysis. After a few moments, the AI's verdict appeared on the hidden screen: the building would indeed fail, but not for the reasons the Renaissance engineers suspected.

"Your engineers are correct to be concerned," Gary said finally, "but they've identified the wrong problem."

"Explain."

Gary pointed to specific sections of the drawings, channeling ARIA's analysis into language a Renaissance banker could understand. "The foundation itself is adequate for the planned weight. However, the load distribution through the wooden support beams creates stress concentrations at these points." He indicated several joints in the structure. "Under full grain load, these connections will fail progressively, causing a catastrophic collapse."

Lorenzo leaned forward. "And the solution?"

"Redistribute the load paths using diagonal bracing members here, here, and here," Gary said, sketching modifications on the plans. "Additionally, replace these wooden joints with iron reinforcements. The cost will be significant, but far less than rebuilding after a collapse."

"How can you be certain of this analysis?"

Gary looked up from the drawings with what he hoped was confident authority. "Mathematics doesn't lie, Signore. The forces acting on this structure follow predictable laws. Calculate them correctly, and the outcome becomes clear."

Lorenzo studied Gary for a long moment, then called for his head engineer. The man who appeared was gray-haired, weathered, and clearly skeptical about consulting a foreign scholar on architectural matters.

"Maestro Roberto," Lorenzo said, "examine this analysis. Tell me what you think."

Roberto looked over Gary's modifications with the careful attention of someone whose reputation depended on not making mistakes. After several minutes, his expression shifted from skepticism to surprise to something approaching awe.

"Signore," he said slowly, "this approach... it addresses problems I hadn't even considered. The stress analysis on these joints..." He looked up at Gary with new respect. "Where did you learn such advanced principles?"

"Universities in Germania have made significant advances in structural mathematics," Gary replied, which was technically true if you considered MIT to be in Germania and 520 years in the future.

"Remarkable," Roberto muttered, still studying the drawings. "With these modifications, the building would be stronger than most palazzos in Florence."

Lorenzo smiled—genuinely this time. "It seems your reputation is well-deserved, Master Gary. My engineer approves your solution, which means I can proceed with confidence."

"I'm pleased to be of service."

"Indeed you are." Lorenzo walked around his desk and clasped Gary's shoulder. "Tell me, what are your plans in Florence? Do you have permanent patronage?"

Gary felt his heart jump. This was the moment he'd been working toward—the offer of serious patronage from a major Florentine power broker.

"I'm currently... exploring opportunities," he said carefully.

"Then perhaps we should discuss a more formal arrangement. I have many projects that could benefit from your expertise. Architecture, trade route optimization, engineering challenges..." Lorenzo's eyes gleamed with possibility. "A man of your abilities should not be solving carpentry problems for street merchants."

Benedetto, who had been silent during the technical discussion, now spoke up. "Signore, if I may suggest, Master Gary has other commitments that would need to be considered..."

"Of course," Lorenzo said smoothly. "We can discuss terms that accommodate all parties. But first, I'd like to demonstrate Master Gary's capabilities to some associates of mine. There's a gathering tomorrow evening—influential people who appreciate intellectual achievement."

"What kind of gathering?" Gary asked.

"A dinner party. Some of Florence's finest minds discussing current challenges. Your presence would be... enlightening for everyone involved."

Gary looked at Benedetto, who nodded enthusiastically. This was exactly the kind of opportunity they'd been hoping for—access to the highest levels of Florentine society.

"I'd be honored to attend," Gary said.

"Excellent. Benedetto can provide you with appropriate attire." Lorenzo returned to his desk and made a note on a piece of parchment. "One more thing, Master Gary. My guests will undoubtedly want to test your abilities. Are you prepared to demonstrate your skills under... scrutiny?"

"What kind of demonstration?"

Lorenzo smiled. "Nothing too challenging. Perhaps some astronomical calculations, engineering problems, mathematical puzzles. The sort of intellectual entertainment that impresses educated people."

Gary felt ARIA shift slightly in his satchel, and he could imagine the warnings his AI would be displaying if he could see the screen. But he was feeling confident now. He'd just solved a complex structural engineering problem and earned the admiration of one of Florence's most powerful men. How hard could it be to impress a few dinner guests?

"I'm ready for any challenge," Gary said.

Lorenzo's smile widened. "I was hoping you'd say that."

## Chapter 9: The Dinner Party From Hell

Benedetto spent most of the next day in a state of barely controlled panic, running around Florence trying to acquire clothing appropriate for dining with what he kept calling "the most dangerous people in Italy."

"Dangerous how?" Gary asked as Benedetto held up various doublets and tried to determine which one would be least likely to get them both killed.

"Dangerous like sharp knives hidden in silk purses," Benedetto replied, settling on a dark blue doublet with silver trim. "These are not just wealthy men, Gary. They are the men who decide which other men get to remain wealthy. Or alive."

"You're being dramatic."

"Am I? Let me tell you who will be at this dinner." Benedetto began counting on his fingers. "Lorenzo Capponi, obviously. Probably his cousin Alessandro, who once had a rival banker's entire family exiled for using the wrong color ink on a contract. Giuliano de' Medici, who is charming, intelligent, and rumored to have personally strangled a man for looking at his wife incorrectly."

Gary tried on the doublet, which fit reasonably well. "Anyone else I should worry about?"

"Oh, certainly. Francesco Soderini, who can calculate interest rates in his head faster than most men can count to ten, and who has been known to bankrupt entire cities for entertainment. Possibly Niccolò Machiavelli, if he's in town—you may have heard of him?"

Gary hadn't, but nodded anyway.

"And that's just the politicians and bankers," Benedetto continued cheerfully. "There will also be artists, scholars, engineers—all of them brilliant, all of them competitive, and all of them looking for any opportunity to prove they're smarter than everyone else in the room."

"ARIA," Gary whispered that evening while getting dressed, "please tell me you're ready for this."

"Gary," ARIA replied from its hiding place, "I've been analyzing the social dynamics you're about to enter. These Renaissance dinner parties were intellectual combat zones. People's careers lived or died based on their performance at these gatherings."

"But I have you. We can handle anything they throw at us."

"That's what I'm afraid of. The more impressive you are, the more attention you'll attract. And attention from powerful people in this era can be... problematic."

Gary adjusted his cap and checked his appearance in Benedetto's small mirror. He looked like a proper Renaissance scholar—educated but not wealthy, serious but approachable.

"Don't worry so much," he told ARIA. "I've got this figured out."

The Capponi palazzo looked even more impressive in the evening, lit by torches that created dramatic shadows on its carved facade. Gary and Benedetto were led through corridors that seemed designed to make visitors feel simultaneously awed and slightly intimidated, past artwork that belonged in museums and furniture that probably cost more than small kingdoms.

They arrived at a dining hall that could have hosted a small army, though tonight it held only a single long table set for perhaps a dozen people. The other guests were already seated, engaged in animated conversation that stopped when Gary entered.

"Gentlemen," Lorenzo announced, "allow me to present Master Gary of Germania, the scholar I mentioned. Master Gary, please join us."

Gary found himself seated between a man who introduced himself as Francesco Soderini and another who turned out to be Giuliano de' Medici—yes, that Giuliano de' Medici, the one who supposedly strangled people.

"So," Giuliano said conversationally as the first course was served, "Lorenzo tells us you can predict eclipses with mathematical precision."

"Among other things," Gary replied, trying to sound modest while cutting what appeared to be some kind of bird into manageable pieces.

"How refreshing," said a thin man across the table who had been introduced as Niccolò Machiavelli. "Another scholar who claims to have discovered the secrets of celestial mechanics."

There was something in Machiavelli's tone that suggested this wasn't entirely a compliment.

"I don't claim to have discovered anything," Gary said carefully. "I simply apply mathematical principles that are... well-established in my homeland."

"Germania has made remarkable advances recently," Francesco observed. "Strange that we hear so little about these developments."

Gary felt the first stirring of unease. These men weren't just wealthy—they were smart, educated, and clearly skeptical about his claims.

"Knowledge travels slowly," he said. "Especially across the Alps."

"Indeed," Machiavelli smiled. "Tell me, Master Gary, what do you think of the current political situation in Italy?"

This was exactly the kind of question Gary had been dreading. His knowledge of Renaissance politics was limited to half-remembered history classes and a general sense that everyone was always fighting someone else about something.

"It's... complex," he said, buying time while trying to think of something intelligent to add.

"Complex," Machiavelli repeated. "How diplomatic. And how would you resolve this complexity, if you were, say, advising a prince?"

Gary realized he was being tested, and not just on his mathematical abilities. These men wanted to know if he was worth their time, or if he was just another traveling fraud with more confidence than competence.

Time to take a calculated risk.

"Before offering political advice," Gary said slowly, "I would need to analyze the underlying mathematical structures of power distribution and resource allocation."

The table went quiet. Machiavelli raised an eyebrow.

"Mathematical structures of power?"

"Everything can be quantified," Gary continued, warming to his theme and hoping ARIA would be able to help if things got too specific. "Political stability, economic efficiency, military effectiveness—they all follow mathematical laws. Most advisors rely on intuition and tradition. I prefer to rely on numbers."

"Show us," Giuliano said simply.

"I beg your pardon?"

"Show us these mathematical laws. Give us an example."

Gary's mind raced. He needed something impressive but not too specific, mathematical but not obviously anachronistic.

"Very well," he said, settling on an approach that would let ARIA do most of the work. "Francesco, you mentioned earlier that you're involved in banking?"

"Among other activities, yes."

"Then you understand compound interest calculations?"

"Naturally."

"Good. Now, imagine you could apply similar mathematical principles to political power. Instead of money earning interest over time, consider how influence compounds when properly invested."

Gary was making this up as he went along, but he could see that the men around the table were listening intently.

"For example," he continued, "if you want to predict the stability of an alliance between two city-states, you calculate the rate of benefit each party derives, account for the decay function of trust over time, and factor in the external pressure variables..."

He started sketching equations on the tablecloth with his finger, channeling mathematical concepts that ARIA was feeding him through subtle vibrations in his pocket.

"This predicts that the current Papal alliance with France will dissolve within eighteen months," Gary concluded, "due to incompatible benefit structures and accelerating trust decay."

The silence at the table was absolute.

"Extraordinary," Francesco murmured. "You're suggesting we can predict political outcomes with the same precision we use for financial calculations?"

"Why not? People think politics is unpredictable because they don't understand the underlying mathematics. But human behavior follows patterns, and patterns can be quantified."

Machiavelli leaned forward, his eyes bright with interest. "And this mathematical approach—you learned this in Germania?"

"The basic principles, yes. Though I've developed many of the applications myself."

"Remarkable," Giuliano said. "If such techniques were reliable, they would be worth... considerable investment."

Gary felt a thrill of success. He'd managed to turn a potentially disastrous political discussion into another demonstration of his supposed genius, and the most powerful men in Florence were clearly impressed.

That's when Lorenzo cleared his throat.

"Gentlemen, I believe Master Gary has given us a tantalizing preview of his capabilities. But perhaps we should test them more thoroughly?"

"What kind of test?" Gary asked, though he suspected he wasn't going to like the answer.

Lorenzo smiled. "Nothing too challenging. I've prepared a small demonstration of your predictive abilities."

A servant appeared with a leather portfolio, from which Lorenzo extracted several sheets of parchment covered with diagrams and numbers.

"These are engineering challenges currently facing the city of Florence," Lorenzo explained. "The new bridge construction, the cathedral dome repairs, the water supply project for the Santo Spirito district. Each project has encountered problems that our finest engineers have been unable to solve."

He spread the documents on the table where everyone could see them.

"If your mathematical approach is as revolutionary as you suggest, surely you can provide solutions that have eluded our local experts?"

Gary looked down at the engineering drawings and felt his confidence evaporate. These weren't simple geometry problems or basic structural calculations. These were complex, real-world engineering challenges that would require detailed analysis of materials, environmental factors, and construction techniques that were probably unique to Renaissance Italy.

In other words, exactly the kind of problems that would expose him as a fraud if he got them wrong.

"These look quite complex," he said carefully. "I would normally need time to study the specifications, examine the actual sites..."

"Of course," Lorenzo said smoothly. "But surely a preliminary analysis? Something to demonstrate your methods?"

Gary realized he was trapped. If he refused the challenge, he'd look like a fraud. If he accepted and failed, he'd be exposed as a fraud. And if he succeeded too dramatically, he might raise questions about how a traveling scholar could outperform the best engineers in Florence.

He looked down at the drawings, his mind racing through options. None of them were good.

That's when he noticed something that made his blood run cold.

Sitting at the far end of the table, partially hidden in shadow, was a man who hadn't been introduced. A man who was watching Gary with the kind of intensity that suggested professional interest rather than casual curiosity.

A man whose clothing, posture, and general demeanor screamed "I work for people who ask very pointed questions about suspicious foreigners."

Gary was pretty sure he'd just discovered that Renaissance dinner parties came with their own version of secret police.

And they wanted him to prove he was a genius.

Right now.

## Chapter 10: The Performance of a Lifetime

You know that moment when you realize you've been walking confidently toward what you thought was a friendly gathering, only to discover you've actually walked into a trap designed by people who are much smarter and much more dangerous than you anticipated? Gary was having that moment, amplified by the fact that he was surrounded by Renaissance Italy's version of a shark tank, and the sharks were all watching him with the focused attention of predators who had just smelled blood in the water.

"The bridge project," Gary said finally, pointing to what looked like the simplest of the three engineering challenges. "May I examine this one first?"

Lorenzo nodded approvingly. "An excellent choice. The Ponte alle Grazie has developed structural problems that threaten its stability. Our engineers have proposed several solutions, but none have proven satisfactory."

Gary studied the drawings while subtly activating ARIA in his satchel. The AI's camera could make out enough detail through the leather to begin analyzing the structural plans, but Gary knew he needed to buy time for a complete assessment.

"The original construction date?" he asked.

"Completed in 1237," Francesco replied. "Stone piers, wooden superstructure, fourteen arches."

Gary nodded thoughtfully, channeling the information to ARIA while maintaining the appearance of deep contemplation. "And the specific failure points?"

"The seventh and ninth piers show signs of settling," Giuliano said. "The wooden deck shows stress fractures at three locations. Our engineers fear catastrophic failure during the next major flood."

As Gary asked more technical questions, ARIA processed the structural data and historical information, building a comprehensive analysis of the bridge's problems. But there was another issue: the mysterious man at the end of the table was taking notes about Gary's questions and responses.

"The mathematical approach would begin with load distribution analysis," Gary said, starting to sketch calculations on the parchment. "But first, we need to understand the soil composition beneath the failing piers."

He was stalling while ARIA completed its analysis, but the technical questions also served another purpose—they made him sound like he knew what he was doing.

"Clay and river silt," Lorenzo provided. "The foundation depth varies from eight to twelve braccia."

ARIA's screen finally displayed its conclusion: the bridge problems were caused by differential settling due to inconsistent foundation depths, exacerbated by changes in river flow patterns over the past century. The AI even provided specific recommendations for stabilization.

"I see the issue," Gary announced, feeling a surge of confidence as he began transcribing ARIA's analysis. "The problem isn't structural weakness—it's foundation inconsistency creating uneven stress distribution."

He started drawing diagrams showing how the settling piers were creating stress concentrations in the wooden superstructure.

"The solution requires underpinning the affected piers with stone reinforcement, extending their foundations to uniform depth," Gary continued, sketching ARIA's recommended fixes. "Additionally, the wooden deck stress can be relieved by installing tension cables at these specific points..."

As he explained the technical solution, Gary could see the faces around the table shifting from skepticism to surprise to something approaching awe. These weren't just wealthy patrons—they were educated men who understood engineering, and they could recognize a sophisticated analysis when they heard one.

"Fascinating," murmured a gray-haired man who had been introduced as Maestro Giuliano da Sangallo, Florence's chief architect. "This approach to foundation stabilization... where did you encounter such techniques?"

"The mathematical principles are universal," Gary replied smoothly. "Once you understand load distribution and soil mechanics, the solutions become evident."

"Soil mechanics?" Sangallo leaned forward. "I'm not familiar with this term."

Gary realized he'd just used a phrase that probably wouldn't be invented for another few centuries.

"The... mathematical study of earth and stone behavior under load," he improvised. "How different soil types respond to weight and pressure over time."

"Remarkable," Sangallo said. "Such systematic analysis could revolutionize construction throughout Italy."

The mysterious note-taker at the end of the table looked up from his writing and spoke for the first time.

"Master Gary," he said in a voice that carried the kind of quiet authority that made everyone else shut up, "your knowledge seems remarkably... comprehensive for a traveling scholar."

Every person at the table turned to look at the speaker, and Gary suddenly understood that whoever this man was, he was more important than all the other dangerous people combined.

"I beg your pardon?" Gary said carefully.

"Simply an observation," the man replied with a smile that didn't reach his eyes. "Your analytical methods are quite advanced. Far beyond what we typically see from northern universities."

Gary felt his mouth go dry. "I've been fortunate to study with some exceptional teachers."

"Indeed. And these teachers... they developed these mathematical techniques themselves?"

Gary realized he was being interrogated by someone who was very good at making interrogations sound like casual conversation.

"Knowledge builds upon knowledge," Gary said, hoping he sounded profound rather than evasive. "Each generation advances beyond the previous."

"Certainly," the man agreed. "Though such advances usually take decades or centuries to develop. Your techniques suggest... remarkably rapid progress."

The atmosphere at the table had shifted. The intellectual excitement was still there, but underneath it was a growing sense of tension. Gary could feel everyone watching him, waiting to see how he would respond to this polite but pointed questioning.

"Perhaps," Lorenzo interjected smoothly, "we should examine the other engineering challenges? Master Gary, would you be willing to analyze the cathedral dome repairs?"

Gary knew this was Lorenzo's attempt to deflect the mysterious interrogator's questions, but it also meant more pressure to perform. The cathedral dome was probably Brunelleschi's masterpiece, and any problems with it would be monumentally complex.

"Of course," Gary said, though his confidence was starting to waver.

The dome drawings were incredibly detailed, showing not just the basic structure but also the innovative engineering techniques that had made its construction possible. Gary studied them while ARIA processed the technical specifications, but he could feel the weight of everyone's attention.

"The issue appears to be seasonal movement," Sangallo explained. "The structure expands and contracts with temperature changes, creating stress fractures in the lantern supports."

ARIA quickly provided an analysis: the problem was thermal expansion causing differential movement between the inner and outer dome shells. The solution involved installing flexible joints at key stress points.

But as Gary began explaining the technical fix, he noticed the note-taker watching him with increasing intensity. The man wasn't just listening to Gary's words—he was studying Gary's methods, his timing, his body language.

"Remarkable diagnostic ability," the man said when Gary finished his explanation. "You seem to identify structural problems with unusual... speed."

"Experience teaches efficiency," Gary replied, wishing he could see ARIA's screen to check for warnings about what he was saying.

"Yes, experience," the man repeated thoughtfully. "Tell me, Master Gary, what do you know about the coming political changes in Italy?"

The question hit Gary like a physical blow. This wasn't about engineering anymore—this was someone testing whether Gary had knowledge he shouldn't possess.

"I'm afraid I don't understand the question," Gary said carefully.

"Simply curiosity about your political insights. Earlier, you predicted the dissolution of the Papal alliance with France. Rather specific timing for a mathematical projection."

Gary's heart started pounding. He'd gotten carried away during his demonstration and made a prediction that was probably too detailed, too accurate.

"Mathematical analysis of alliance stability," he said weakly. "Based on observable trend patterns."

"Indeed. And what other trends have you observed?"

Gary realized he was about to be caught in a trap. If he claimed ignorance of future political events, he'd contradict his earlier demonstration. If he made more predictions, he'd risk revealing knowledge that no one in 1505 should possess.

That's when Benedetto, who had been silent throughout the dinner, suddenly knocked over his wine cup.

"Madonna mia!" he exclaimed, jumping up as red wine spread across the white tablecloth. "How clumsy of me!"

The brief commotion gave Gary a moment to think, and he suddenly realized what he needed to do. Instead of trying to deflect more questions, he needed to go on the offensive.

"Signore," he said to the note-taker, "I notice you've been documenting our conversation quite thoroughly. Are you perhaps conducting some kind of evaluation?"

The man smiled—a real smile this time. "Very observant, Master Gary. I am Francesco Guicciardini, and I serve various interested parties who are... curious about unusual visitors to Florence."

Gary felt his blood turn to ice water. He didn't know who Francesco Guicciardini was, but the name sounded official and potentially lethal.

"I hope I haven't said anything to cause concern," Gary said.

"On the contrary," Guicciardini replied. "You've said several things that are quite remarkable. Perhaps we could continue this conversation in a more... private setting?"

Gary looked around the table and realized that what had started as a dinner party demonstration had somehow turned into a recruitment interview. And he was pretty sure that declining the invitation wasn't really an option.

"What kind of private setting?" he asked.

Guicciardini's smile widened. "The kind where we discuss how your remarkable abilities might serve the interests of Florence... and her allies."

Gary looked at Lorenzo, who nodded encouragingly. He looked at Benedetto, who was trying very hard to appear invisible. He looked at all the other powerful men around the table, who were watching this exchange with the fascination of people who knew they were witnessing something significant.

And then he looked down at his satchel, where ARIA was probably displaying warnings and recommendations that he couldn't see.

"I'd be honored to serve Florence in any way I can," Gary said, because what else could he say?

"Excellent," Guicciardini said, closing his notebook with a decisive snap. "Tomorrow morning, then. I'll send a carriage."

As the dinner party began to wind down, Gary realized he'd just agreed to something that was probably going to change his life dramatically. He wasn't sure if it was going to change it for better or worse, but he had a sinking feeling that he'd just volunteered for a job that came with either extraordinary rewards or extraordinary risks.

Possibly both.

## Chapter 11: The Job Interview from the Renaissance

The carriage that arrived the next morning wasn't the kind of vehicle that suggested friendly conversation. It was black, unmarked, and driven by a man whose facial expression suggested he'd learned customer service from gargoyles. When Gary climbed inside, he discovered that the windows were covered with thick curtains that prevented him from seeing where they were going.

"This is either really good or really bad," he whispered to ARIA, who was hidden in his satchel as usual.

"Gary," ARIA replied quietly, "based on my analysis of last night's conversation, I believe you may have attracted the attention of Renaissance Italy's intelligence services."

"We have intelligence services?"

"Every major power in this period employs networks of spies, informants, and analysts. Francesco Guicciardini is one of the most sophisticated political minds of his era."

Gary tried to look out the curtained windows but could see nothing except occasional glimpses of stone buildings. "What does he want with me?"

"Based on your demonstration last night, probably to determine whether you're a valuable asset, a dangerous threat, or both."

The carriage ride lasted about twenty minutes before stopping at what sounded like a busy courtyard. Gary was escorted by silent guards through corridors that smelled like old stone and important decisions, past walls decorated with tapestries that depicted various people doing violent things to other people in historically significant ways.

He was eventually led into a chamber that managed to be both impressive and intimidating. The room was circular, with a domed ceiling decorated with frescoes showing allegorical figures representing Justice, Wisdom, and other concepts that probably had very specific meanings to people who made their living deciding the fates of nations.

Francesco Guicciardini was waiting behind a desk that looked like it had been carved from a single piece of very expensive wood. He was alone, which somehow made him seem more dangerous rather than less.

"Master Gary," he said, gesturing to a chair across from his desk. "Thank you for accepting my invitation."

"Thank you for extending it," Gary replied, though they both knew it hadn't really been an invitation.

"I trust you slept well after our stimulating dinner conversation?"

"Very well, thank you."
