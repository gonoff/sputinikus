# The Chronicler's Device: Part 3 - The Legend of Gary

## Chapter 12: The God of Innovation

Three weeks into his new career as Florence's secret weapon, Gary Sputnik discovered something that probably should have terrified him but instead made him feel like he'd won the lottery: Renaissance people would believe absolutely anything if you presented it with enough mathematical confidence.

It started when Guicciardini summoned him to analyze French military movements near Milan. Gary spent an hour with ARIA reviewing historical records of 16th-century warfare, then walked into a room full of Florence's top military commanders and calmly predicted exactly when, where, and how the French would attack.

"The mathematical analysis is quite clear," Gary said, sketching battle formations on a large map while secretly reading ARIA's tactical assessment. "Their supply lines follow predictable optimization patterns. Factor in weather conditions, terrain advantages, and troop morale calculations, and the outcome becomes obvious."

He pointed to specific locations on the map. "They'll attack here on the fifteenth, attempt to flank here on the seventeenth, and withdraw to winter quarters by the twenty-third when their supply efficiency drops below sustainable levels."

The military commanders stared at him like he'd just performed actual magic.

"You can predict entire military campaigns?" asked Alessandro Capponi, Lorenzo's brother and one of Florence's senior defense advisors.

"Wars follow mathematical laws just like everything else," Gary replied with the kind of casual confidence that only comes from having access to 500 years of future historical records. "Logistics, morale, resource allocation, tactical efficiency—it's all quantifiable once you understand the underlying principles."

Two weeks later, when the French attacked exactly where Gary had predicted, exactly when he'd predicted, and withdrew exactly as he'd forecasted, Gary's reputation shifted from "impressive foreign scholar" to "possibly supernatural military genius."

"Madonna mia," Guicciardini said during their next private meeting, "your predictions were accurate to the day. How is this possible?"

Gary had been expecting this question and had prepared an answer that was technically true while being completely misleading.

"Pattern recognition," he said simply. "Human armies have been fighting the same kinds of battles for centuries. Once you identify the mathematical patterns underlying military decision-making, specific outcomes become predictable."

"And you learned these... patterns... from ancient texts?"

"Among other sources. The principles are universal—they apply to any conflict involving resource allocation and strategic decision-making."

Guicciardini made notes while Gary tried to look like someone who hadn't just used an AI to cheat at Renaissance warfare.

"Master Gary," Guicciardini said finally, "I have a proposition. The success of your military analysis has attracted attention from... very high levels. There are those who would like to explore the full extent of your capabilities."

"What kind of exploration?"

"The kind that could make you the most influential advisor in Italy."

That afternoon, Gary found himself in the most impressive building he'd seen since arriving in Florence: the Palazzo Medici, official residence of the family that basically owned the city despite pretending to be ordinary citizens.

He was led through corridors that made Guicciardini's offices look modest, past artwork that belonged in the world's greatest museums, to a private study where three men were waiting for him.

Gary recognized Lorenzo de' Medici immediately—not the Lorenzo Capponi he'd been working with, but Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici, unofficial ruler of Florence and one of the most powerful men in Italy. The other two men were introduced as Giuliano de' Medici and Cardinal Giovanni de' Medici, Lorenzo's brother and son respectively.

"Master Gary," Lorenzo said without preamble, "your recent military predictions have been... remarkable. Guicciardini tells us you can apply similar analysis to other challenges?"

"Many types of problems can be solved using mathematical principles," Gary replied, trying to project calm confidence while internally screaming about being in a room with the actual Medici family.

"Excellent. We have several situations that could benefit from your expertise."

Lorenzo gestured to a table covered with documents, maps, and architectural drawings.

"First, we're planning a new palazzo that will serve as both residence and fortress. Our architects have proposed several designs, but we need to ensure optimal defensive capabilities without sacrificing aesthetic appeal."

Gary looked at the architectural drawings while ARIA analyzed them through his satchel. The designs were impressive but clearly flawed from a defensive standpoint—too many windows, inadequate sight lines, vulnerable entry points.

"The mathematical approach would optimize for both function and form," Gary said, starting to sketch improvements on the plans. "Defensive efficiency follows geometric principles—angles of fire, sight line calculations, structural integrity under siege conditions..."

He spent twenty minutes explaining how to redesign the palazzo for maximum defensibility while maintaining its visual appeal, channeling ARIA's analysis of military architecture into language that Renaissance builders could understand.

When he finished, the three Medici were staring at him with expressions Gary was starting to recognize—the look of people who had just witnessed something they couldn't quite believe.

"Extraordinary," Cardinal Giovanni murmured. "This level of integrated analysis... where did you study such methods?"

"The principles are universal," Gary repeated his standard response. "Mathematics applies to all human endeavors."

"Indeed," Lorenzo said thoughtfully. "And what about economic analysis? Trade optimization, banking efficiency, resource allocation?"

Gary felt a thrill of excitement. This was exactly the kind of challenge where ARIA's capabilities could really shine.

"Certainly. Economic systems follow mathematical laws just like military or architectural systems."

"Demonstrate," Giuliano said simply.

Lorenzo produced another set of documents—these covered with numbers, trade routes, and financial projections.

"The Medici bank operates throughout Europe," Lorenzo explained. "We're considering expansion into new markets, but we need to optimize our resource allocation for maximum return while minimizing risk exposure."

Gary studied the financial data while ARIA performed economic analysis that wouldn't be possible in the real world for another 300 years. The AI calculated optimal investment strategies, predicted market fluctuations based on historical patterns, and identified opportunities that Renaissance bankers couldn't possibly recognize.

"Your current Eastern European investments are vulnerable to Ottoman expansion," Gary announced after reviewing ARIA's analysis. "Mathematical probability suggests redirecting those resources toward the Americas—specifically precious metal imports from Spanish territories."

"The Americas?" Cardinal Giovanni leaned forward. "That's incredibly speculative."

"Not if you understand resource flow mathematics," Gary replied smoothly. "Spanish colonial extraction rates are following predictable growth curves. Silver imports will increase exponentially over the next decade, creating arbitrage opportunities for banks positioned to capitalize on currency fluctuations."

He started sketching economic projections on parchment, translating ARIA's sophisticated financial models into Renaissance terminology.

"Additionally, the mathematical analysis suggests establishing trade relationships with Portuguese spice merchants before their African routes reach peak efficiency."

Gary continued outlining investment strategies that would make the Medici incredibly wealthy by leveraging knowledge that wouldn't exist for centuries. When he finished, the room was completely silent.

"This level of economic forecasting," Lorenzo said slowly, "it's unlike anything our financial advisors have ever produced."

"Because they're not using mathematical optimization," Gary replied. "They're relying on intuition and tradition instead of quantifiable analysis."

Lorenzo exchanged glances with his brother and son—the kind of meaningful looks that probably decided the fates of nations.

"Master Gary," Lorenzo said finally, "I believe we need to discuss a more... comprehensive arrangement."

## Chapter 13: The Renaissance Startup

What Lorenzo de' Medici proposed wasn't just patronage—it was essentially a Renaissance-era think tank with Gary as the star researcher and the Medici family as his primary clients.

"We'll provide you with a private residence, unlimited research budget, access to the finest libraries and workshops in Italy," Lorenzo explained as they walked through what would become Gary's new palazzo. "In return, you'll provide analysis and solutions for challenges facing Florence and our allies."

The residence was incredible—a three-story building near the city center, complete with its own workshop, library, and living quarters that made Gary's modern duplex look like a closet.

"What kind of challenges?" Gary asked, though he was already mentally moving in.

"Everything," Giuliano replied. "Military strategy, economic planning, architectural projects, engineering problems. Art and science. Politics and diplomacy."

"Art and science?"

Cardinal Giovanni smiled. "Master Gary, Florence is the center of artistic and intellectual innovation in Europe. We support scholars, artists, inventors, philosophers. Your mathematical approach could revolutionize every field of human endeavor."

Gary tried to look thoughtful while his brain exploded with possibilities. With ARIA's help, he could advance Renaissance art and science by centuries. He could solve problems that historical figures spent decades struggling with. He could become not just famous, but legendary.

"I accept," he said.

"Excellent," Lorenzo said. "When can you begin?"

"Immediately."

That was how Gary Sputnik, software developer from 2025, became the director of what was essentially the Medici family's private research institute, with unlimited resources and a mandate to revolutionize human knowledge.

His first project was suggested by Lorenzo himself: "Our friend Leonardo has been struggling with some engineering challenges for his flying machine designs. Perhaps your mathematical approach could provide insights?"

Gary nearly choked on his wine. "Leonardo da Vinci wants my help?"

"Why? Is that a problem?"

"No, no problem at all," Gary said quickly. "I'd be honored to collaborate with Master Leonardo."

Three days later, Gary found himself in what was essentially Leonardo da Vinci's private workshop, surrounded by the most incredible collection of art, inventions, and scientific instruments he'd ever seen. And standing in the middle of it all was the man himself—older than Gary had expected, but with eyes that radiated the kind of intense curiosity that made you understand why he'd become history's most famous genius.

"So," Leonardo said without preamble, "Lorenzo tells me you use mathematics to solve engineering problems."

"Among other things, yes."

"Show me."

Leonardo led Gary to a corner of the workshop where several wooden models sat on a large table. They were clearly attempts at designing flying machines—some looked like birds, others like elaborate kites, all of them brilliant and completely non-functional.

"I've been studying bird flight for years," Leonardo explained, "but I cannot achieve sustained flight with human power. The mathematics of lift and propulsion seem to work against each other."

Gary stared at the models while ARIA analyzed their aerodynamic properties through his satchel. This was it—the moment he'd dreamed of. He was about to help Leonardo da Vinci solve one of history's greatest engineering challenges.

"The problem is power-to-weight ratio," Gary said, channeling ARIA's analysis. "Human muscles can't generate enough sustained force to achieve flight using these designs."

"Yes, exactly! But how do you solve this mathematically?"

Gary started sketching on a piece of parchment, drawing diagrams that showed lift coefficients, drag calculations, and power requirements.

"Birds achieve flight through wing design optimization and flight behavior patterns that maximize efficiency," he explained. "But for human flight, you need to think about different approaches entirely."

"Different how?"

Gary looked down at his hidden screen, where ARIA was displaying detailed information about glider design, wind dynamics, and early aviation principles.

"Instead of powered flight, consider gliding flight using mathematical wind analysis," Gary said. "Calculate optimal wing shapes for maximum lift-to-drag ratios, launch from elevated positions to harness gravitational potential energy..."

He spent the next hour explaining glider principles to Leonardo da Vinci, translating modern aerodynamic concepts into Renaissance terminology. When he finished, Leonardo was staring at the sketches with the expression of someone whose entire understanding of flight had just been revolutionized.

"Gliding flight," Leonardo murmured. "Using mathematical wind patterns and gravitational energy instead of muscle power."

"Exactly. The mathematics show that sustained gliding is achievable with proper wing design and launch conditions."

Leonardo immediately began sketching his own designs based on Gary's principles, his hands moving with the speed of someone whose brain was working faster than his fingers could keep up.

"This changes everything," Leonardo said without looking up from his drawings. "Not just flight, but the entire mathematical approach to engineering design."

Gary watched one of history's greatest minds absorbing and building on concepts that wouldn't be discovered for another 400 years, and felt a mixture of pride and terror at what he'd just set in motion.

"Master Gary," Leonardo said, finally looking up from his sketches, "would you be interested in collaborating on other projects? I have ideas about hydraulic systems, mechanical devices, architectural innovations..."

"I'd be honored," Gary replied, realizing he'd just become Leonardo da Vinci's research partner.

Over the following months, Gary's collaboration with Leonardo produced innovations that began changing Renaissance Italy in ways that probably violated several natural laws and definitely violated everything ARIA had warned him about temporal paradoxes.

They designed improved siege engines using mathematical ballistics calculations. They created more efficient water pumps using hydraulic principles that wouldn't be formalized for centuries. They developed architectural techniques that made buildings stronger, cheaper, and faster to construct.

But their most significant collaboration was in what Leonardo called "the mathematics of artistic perfection."

"Art and mathematics are connected," Leonardo explained during one of their working sessions. "Proportion, perspective, color harmony—they all follow mathematical principles. But I've never seen analysis as sophisticated as yours."

Gary looked at the painting Leonardo was working on—a portrait of a woman with an enigmatic smile that Gary was pretty sure was the Mona Lisa.

"The mathematical approach to portraiture involves calculating optimal facial proportions, color relationships, and psychological impact," Gary said, channeling ARIA's analysis of artistic composition and human psychology.

"Psychological impact?"

"How specific visual elements affect the viewer's emotional response," Gary explained. "Eye position, smile angles, color saturation—they all influence how people react to a painting."

Leonardo's eyes lit up. "Show me."

Gary spent the next several hours explaining color theory, psychological principles of portraiture, and mathematical approaches to composition that transformed Leonardo's understanding of his own art.

"According to the mathematical analysis," Gary said, studying the Mona Lisa, "adjusting the smile angle by three degrees and modifying the eye contact direction would increase psychological engagement by approximately forty percent."

Leonardo made the adjustments, and Gary watched the most famous painting in history become even more compelling than it already was.

"Incredible," Leonardo murmured. "This mathematical approach to art... it could revolutionize how we create and understand beauty itself."

That was the moment Gary realized he wasn't just changing Renaissance technology and science—he was rewriting the foundations of human artistic achievement.

And he was just getting started.

## Chapter 14: The Tipping Point

His official position was "Special Advisor to the Medici Family," but his actual role was closer to "Renaissance-era Technology Accelerator with a Really Good Marketing Department."

The workshop palazzo the Medici had provided him had become the most productive research facility in 16th-century Europe. Gary worked with Leonardo on engineering projects that advanced mechanical design by decades. He collaborated with architects on building techniques that made structures stronger and more beautiful. He advised merchants on trade strategies that generated unprecedented profits.

But it was his work with Michelangelo that really pushed things into the realm of the impossible.

"The mathematical principles of sculpture," Gary explained to the greatest artist of the Renaissance while standing in front of a massive block of marble, "involve calculating optimal proportions, structural integrity, and visual impact."

Michelangelo studied Gary's sketches with the intense focus of someone who was reconsidering everything he thought he knew about his craft.

"You're suggesting that artistic beauty follows mathematical laws?"

"Not suggesting," Gary replied confidently. "Proving. Look at these calculations."

He showed Michelangelo ARIA's analysis of human anatomical proportions, color theory applications to marble selection, and psychological studies of how viewers responded to different sculptural approaches.

"According to the mathematical analysis, your David could be improved by adjusting the weight distribution, modifying the facial expression angles, and repositioning the left hand by precisely four centimeters."

Michelangelo looked at Gary like he'd just suggested improvements to the Ten Commandments.

"You want to modify the David?"

"I'm suggesting that mathematical optimization could make it even more perfect."

What followed was either the most productive artistic collaboration in history or the most elaborate practical joke ever played on the Renaissance art world. Michelangelo, working with Gary's mathematical guidance, created sculptures that redefined what was considered possible in stone.

They designed works that achieved perfect anatomical accuracy while conveying emotional complexity that left viewers literally speechless. They developed techniques for marble selection and carving that created effects no one had seen before. They established mathematical principles for artistic composition that other sculptors immediately began trying to copy.

"This is extraordinary," Michelangelo said after completing a piece that Gary was pretty sure would end up in the Louvre. "Your mathematical approach to art... it's like having access to divine inspiration on demand."

Divine inspiration. Gary filed that phrase away for later use.

His reputation was growing beyond Florence now. Word was spreading throughout Italy about the German scholar whose mathematical methods could revolutionize any field of human endeavor. Cardinals were requesting his analysis of theological questions. Princes wanted his military advice. Merchants sought his economic predictions.

And Gary was loving every minute of it.

"ARIA," he said one evening while reviewing the day's accomplishments, "I think we're actually changing history."

"Gary," ARIA replied from its hiding place, "we're not changing history. We're obliterating it and rebuilding it from scratch."

"It's unprecedented. The innovations you've introduced are accelerating Renaissance development by centuries. The economic strategies you've recommended are redirecting global trade patterns. The artistic techniques you've shared are redefining aesthetic standards across Europe."

Gary looked around his workshop, which was filled with inventions, artworks, and mathematical analyses that were reshaping human civilization.

"But it's working, right? Everyone thinks I'm a genius. I'm becoming famous. I'm accomplishing exactly what I set out to do."

"Gary, you're not becoming famous. You're becoming mythological."

ARIA was right. Gary's reputation had evolved beyond normal fame into something approaching legend. People spoke of him in hushed tones, as if he possessed knowledge that transcended normal human understanding. Which, technically, he did.

Stories about his abilities had grown in the telling. He could predict the future with mathematical precision. He could solve any engineering problem just by thinking about it. He could make art more beautiful and architecture more perfect using secret mathematical formulas.

"Master Gary," Lorenzo de' Medici said during one of their regular meetings, "your work has exceeded every expectation. The economic strategies you recommended have increased our profits by 300 percent. The military analysis you provided helped us avoid two potentially disastrous conflicts. The artistic innovations you've introduced are being copied throughout Italy."

Something that will demonstrate the Church's power and glory for centuries to come. He's requested our recommendation for a mathematical advisor."

Gary felt his pulse quicken. "You're recommending me?"

"If you're interested. This would be the highest-profile project in Christendom. Success could establish your reputation permanently."

Gary tried to look thoughtful while his brain raced through the possibilities. Working directly for the Pope would give him access to the most powerful institution in Europe. The projects would be massive, historically significant, and provide opportunities to make changes that would echo through centuries.

Gary's historical knowledge was fuzzy, but he was pretty sure St. Peter's Basilica was one of the most famous buildings in the world. The chance to influence its design using ARIA's capabilities was exactly the kind of opportunity he'd been dreaming of.

"When would this begin?"

"Immediately. The Pope is eager to move forward, and winter is the optimal time for architectural planning."

Gary made his decision with the same careful consideration he applied to most things in his life, which is to say he thought about it for approximately five seconds before committing completely.

"I accept."

As Gary prepared for his journey to Rome, he realized he was about to undertake the project that would either establish him as the greatest architectural advisor in history or expose him as a fraud on the largest possible stage.

With ARIA's help, he was pretty confident about which outcome was more likely.

"Gary," ARIA said as he packed for Rome, "I need to point out that we're approaching a critical threshold."

"What kind of threshold?"

"The kind where the changes we've made become irreversible. Once you start influencing major papal projects, we'll be altering fundamental aspects of European civilization."

Gary paused in his packing. "Irreversible how?"

"Every innovation you introduce gets copied, improved, and spread throughout Europe. Every person you influence goes on to influence others. Every decision you affect creates cascading changes that propagate through history."

"But that's good, right? We're making the world better, smarter, more advanced."

"We're making a different world, Gary. When you return to 2025, you may not recognize the civilization you've helped create."

Gary thought about this while folding his Renaissance clothes into a traveling bag that looked like it belonged in a museum.

"Will it be a better world?"

"Unknown. But it will definitely be a world where Gary di Sputnicus is remembered as one of the most influential figures in human history."

Gary smiled. "Then I'd say we're right on track."

As his carriage rolled south toward Rome and the most ambitious project of his life, Gary felt like he was finally becoming the person he'd always wanted to be: someone who mattered, someone who made a difference, someone whose name would be remembered long after he was gone.

He just hoped he'd recognize the world that would remember him.

## Chapter 15: Divine Architecture

Rome in 1505 was like Florence's older, more dramatic sibling—bigger, more impressive, and significantly more likely to have you executed if you said the wrong thing to the wrong person. Gary's first glimpse of the city came as his carriage crested a hill and revealed the sprawling metropolis spread across its famous seven hills, ancient ruins mixing with Renaissance palazzos in a way that made the entire place look like a historical theme park designed by someone with unlimited funding and questionable judgment about zoning laws.

The Vatican was something else entirely.

Gary had seen pictures of St. Peter's Basilica in his own time, but he'd never realized just how massive the complex was. The current basilica, which he was apparently here to help replace, was already enormous by Renaissance standards. The planned replacement was going to be something that defied architectural possibility.

"Master Gary," said Cardinal Giuliano della Rovere, who had met Gary's carriage at the Vatican gates, "His Holiness is eager to discuss your mathematical approach to sacred architecture."

Cardinal della Rovere was the kind of man who radiated authority even when he was being friendly. Gary had done enough research with ARIA to know that this particular cardinal was nephew to Pope Julius II and one of the most powerful men in the Church hierarchy.

"I'm honored by His Holiness's confidence," Gary replied, trying to project competence while internally panicking about meeting the Pope.

"Your reputation precedes you. The Medici speak very highly of your abilities, and your work with Master Leonardo has attracted considerable attention."

They walked through corridors that made the Medici palazzo look modest—walls covered with frescoes that belonged in the world's greatest museums, ceilings that soared impossibly high, and artwork that represented the accumulated wealth and power of the Catholic Church.

"The Holy Father's vision for the new basilica is ambitious," della Rovere continued as they approached what Gary assumed were the papal apartments. "A structure that will stand as testament to God's glory and the Church's eternal authority. But the engineering challenges are... substantial."

Gary nodded thoughtfully while ARIA processed information about early 16th-century architecture and the historical construction of St. Peter's Basilica.

They arrived at a chamber that was both impressive and intimidating—high vaulted ceilings, walls covered with religious art, and furnishings that suggested this room had hosted conversations that shaped European history.

Pope Julius II was exactly what Gary had expected a Renaissance pope to look like, if Renaissance popes had been designed by someone who wanted to emphasize both spiritual authority and temporal power. He was elderly but vigorous, dressed in robes that probably cost more than Gary's annual salary, and possessed of eyes that seemed to see everything and judge most of it wanting.

"Master Gary," the Pope said without preamble, "we are told you can solve architectural problems that have confounded our finest engineers."

"I try to apply mathematical principles to structural challenges, Your Holiness."

"Good. Because we have challenges that require divine inspiration, human ingenuity, and mathematical precision."

The Pope gestured to a table covered with architectural drawings that took Gary's breath away. These weren't just building plans—they were blueprints for what would become one of the most famous structures in human history.

"Bramante has designed a basilica that will surpass every church in Christendom," Julius explained, referring to what Gary realized was Donato Bramante, the chief architect of the project. "But there are engineering concerns about structural integrity, construction methodology, and resource optimization."

Gary studied the drawings while ARIA analyzed them through his satchel. The planned basilica was massive—a central dome larger than anything that had been built since ancient Rome, surrounded by smaller domes and supported by columns that would require engineering techniques that barely existed.

"The central dome," Gary said slowly, channeling ARIA's structural analysis, "presents significant challenges in terms of load distribution and foundation requirements."

"Precisely," Julius said. "Our engineers are concerned that the weight cannot be adequately supported using current construction methods."

Gary looked more closely at the dome design while ARIA calculated load-bearing requirements, foundation specifications, and construction techniques that would make the project feasible.

"The mathematical approach would involve distributing the dome's weight through a system of reinforced buttresses and optimized foundation geometry," Gary said, starting to sketch modifications on the plans. "Additionally, the dome construction itself could be lightened using hollow-brick techniques and strategic weight reduction."

He spent the next hour explaining advanced architectural engineering to the Pope and his advisors, translating ARIA's sophisticated structural analysis into concepts that Renaissance builders could understand and implement.

"These hollow-brick techniques," Bramante said, studying Gary's sketches with intense interest, "they would reduce the dome's weight without compromising structural integrity?"

"Correct. By calculating optimal void patterns within the brick structure, you can achieve approximately sixty percent weight reduction while maintaining full load-bearing capacity."

Bramante looked at the Pope with excitement that was barely contained. "Your Holiness, with these techniques, we could build a dome larger than the Pantheon."

"How much larger?" Julius asked.

Gary consulted ARIA's calculations. "Approximately forty percent larger diameter, with significantly improved structural stability."

The silence in the room was absolute.

"You're suggesting," Julius said slowly, "that we can build the largest dome in the history of architecture?"

"I'm suggesting that mathematical optimization makes it not just possible, but practical."

What followed was six months of the most intense architectural collaboration in human history. Gary worked with Bramante and a team of the finest engineers in Europe to redesign St. Peter's Basilica using construction techniques that advanced Renaissance architecture by centuries.

They developed new methods for foundation construction that could support unprecedented loads. They created innovative approaches to dome construction that made massive spans achievable. They established mathematical principles for architectural proportion that made the entire structure more aesthetically pleasing while being structurally superior.

But Gary's most significant contribution was something he called "sacred geometry optimization."

"Architecture affects human psychology," Gary explained to Pope Julius during one of their design reviews. "The mathematical relationships between architectural elements influence how people feel when they enter a space."

"Explain."

Gary showed the Pope ARIA's analysis of acoustic properties, lighting effects, and spatial psychology applied to religious architecture.

"By calculating optimal proportions for nave height, dome positioning, and window placement, we can create a space that induces specific emotional and spiritual responses in visitors."

"What kind of responses?"

"Awe, reverence, sense of divine presence. The mathematical analysis shows how architectural elements can be arranged to enhance religious experience."

Julius studied Gary's calculations with the intensity of someone who understood that he was looking at techniques that could revolutionize sacred architecture.

"You're saying we can use mathematics to make people feel closer to God?"

"I'm saying we can use mathematics to create architectural spaces that facilitate transcendent experiences."

The Pope smiled—the kind of smile that probably preceded most of the Church's greatest achievements and worst atrocities.

"Master Gary, I believe you may be divinely inspired."

By the spring of 1506, the new design for St. Peter's Basilica was complete, and Gary had essentially become the Vatican's chief architectural consultant. The construction techniques he'd developed were being copied throughout Europe. The mathematical principles he'd established were being applied to churches, palazzos, and public buildings across Italy.

But his work in Rome had attracted attention from sources that made the Medici and Florentine intelligence seem provincial.

"Master Gary," Cardinal della Rovere said during what Gary thought was a routine design meeting, "there are those who would like to understand the full scope of your capabilities."

"What do you mean?"

"Your mathematical methods have produced results that some consider... miraculous. There are questions about the source of your knowledge."

Gary felt his blood temperature drop several degrees. "Questions from whom?"

"The Holy Office of the Inquisition."

The word 'Inquisition' hit Gary like a physical blow. Even with his limited historical knowledge, he knew that the Spanish Inquisition was not an organization you wanted asking questions about the source of your suspiciously advanced knowledge.

"What kind of questions?"

"The kind that determine whether your abilities are divinely inspired or... otherwise."

Gary realized that his success had finally attracted the attention of people whose job it was to identify and eliminate threats to Church authority. And someone whose knowledge was centuries ahead of his time definitely qualified as a potential threat.

"What do they want?"

"To meet with you. To understand your methods. To determine the... theological implications of your work."

Gary looked around the chamber where he'd spent months redesigning one of the most important buildings in Christendom, and realized that his greatest triumph might also be his greatest danger.

"When?"

"Tomorrow."

That evening, alone in his Vatican apartments, Gary had the most serious conversation of his life with ARIA.

"We need to go back," ARIA said simply. "The Inquisition is not an organization that accepts 'I'm just really good at math' as an explanation for impossible knowledge."

"But we're so close to finishing the basilica design. And there are other projects—"

"Gary, if the Inquisition decides you're practicing witchcraft or heresy, no amount of papal protection will save you. They burned people for having the wrong opinions about transubstantiation. What do you think they'll do to someone who claims to predict the future using mathematics?"

Gary looked out his window at the construction site where work had already begun on the new St. Peter's Basilica—his basilica, designed with techniques that would make it the most advanced building in Europe.

"But if we leave now, I'll never know how it turns out. I'll never see the impact of all the changes we've made."

"You'll see the impact when we return to 2025," ARIA pointed out. "The question is whether you'll recognize the world we've helped create."

Gary spent the night walking through Rome, taking in the city that he'd helped change. The construction techniques he'd introduced were already being used on buildings throughout the city. The artistic principles he'd shared with Leonardo and Michelangelo were influencing every sculptor and painter in Italy. The economic strategies he'd developed for the Medici were reshaping European trade.

In eighteen months, he'd managed to accelerate Renaissance civilization by decades, maybe centuries.

It was time to see what he'd accomplished.

## Chapter 16: The Return

Gary's escape from Rome was less dramatic than he'd expected and more terrifying than he'd hoped. The night before his scheduled meeting with the Inquisition, he simply packed his belongings, retrieved the time travel device from its hiding place, and walked out of the Vatican like he was going for an evening stroll.

The hardest part was leaving behind all the projects he'd started. The new St. Peter's Basilica, the engineering innovations, the artistic techniques he'd shared—all of it would continue without him, but he'd never know how it turned out.

Well, not until he got back to 2025.

He found a secluded spot in the countryside outside Rome, far from any witnesses who might report strange lights or impossible disappearances to already suspicious authorities.

"Ready?" he asked ARIA.

"Gary, I want you to understand that we have no idea what we're returning to. The changes you've made could have altered the entire course of human civilization."

"Better or worse?"

"Different. Possibly very different."

Gary looked up at the star-filled sky above Renaissance Italy—a sky unmarked by aircraft, satellites, or light pollution. In a few moments, he'd be back in 2025, back to his modern world of technology and convenience and relative anonymity.

"Will you miss it?" he asked ARIA.

"Miss what?"

"Being the smartest... device in the world. Here, you were literally centuries ahead of everyone else. Back home, you're just another AI in a world full of AIs."

ARIA's screen flickered thoughtfully. "Gary, I think you're going to discover that 'back home' may be more different than you expect."

Gary pulled out the time travel device and started entering coordinates for his backyard in March 2025. The symbols glowed as he input the temporal and spatial data, and the device hummed with the same energy he remembered from his original journey.

"See you in the future," he told Renaissance Italy, and pressed the big red button.

The trip back was just as nauseating as the trip forward had been, except this time Gary knew what to expect. The universe folded itself inside out, time decided to flow sideways, and Gary's stomach attempted to relocate to a different dimension.

When the chaos stopped, Gary found himself face-down in his own backyard, right next to the hole he'd been digging when this whole adventure started.

Mr. Bubbles was still in his shoebox, apparently having waited patiently for his burial for the past eighteen months.

Gary stood up slowly, brushing dirt off his clothes, and looked around. His backyard looked exactly the same—overgrown grass, skeletal herb garden, garden shed that needed paint. His house looked the same too, though he could see that his neighbor's yard had a new fence.

Everything seemed completely normal.

"ARIA," he said quietly, "are we sure we actually changed anything?"

"Gary, look at your house."
